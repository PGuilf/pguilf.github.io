Discovery: Understanding the problem

It was quicker and easier to start with internal teams to understand supplier pain points. I interviewed 10 Sedex staff across Member Services and Relationship Management to capture trends and frustrations. Findings were analysed in Airtable and high level findings were prioritised. I found suppliers often felt the SAQ was designed for buyers, not them. The system was slow, guidance was skipped, and people struggled to complete sections requiring input from multiple departments. This section also felt long and overwhelming, especially for users who may not use a computer or device regularly as part of their work.

Supplier User Experience Survey

I sent a Qualtrics survey (translated into multiple languages) to over 10,000 suppliers. Around 3% responded, giving us insights into device use, working patterns, and platform frustrations.

Auditing the SAQ experience

I conducted a UX audit of the current SAQ platform, focusing on visual hierarchy, typography, button behaviour, navigation, and feedback loops. It was clear that reducing cognitive load would be key. I also mapped out user flows to etablish any pain points and digged deeper into Sedex's existing persona's.

Shaping a new SAQ flow

The team focused on redesigning the first 20 questions, a test section to validate changes before scaling. I mapped user journeys and sketched three different low-fidelity wireframes to explored layout, guidance, and progress feedback.
Ideas were informed by research, audit insights, and previous workshops.


Low fidelity options

Moving into low fidelity I landed on three options, each reflecting a different navigation model.

  • Option 1 ‚Äì Bitesize dashboard approach with clear tracking, without sub sections
  • Option 2 ‚Äì Tabbed sections with embedded guidance, with subsections in sections
  • Option 3 ‚Äì Linear form with descriptions and tooltips, with subsections in sections

This was my first opportunity to share something with the internal teams I was working alongside. I was moving rapidly, so getting a low-fidelity version in front of the developers and PM early on was important‚Äîit gave us a chance to align on direction and start discussing feasibility.

Mid-fidelity and sequencing

Reviewed and reorganized SAQ questions, then developed two Figma prototypes based on research, feedback, and competitive analysis.

  • Option 1 was presented in three distinct sections on a dashboard. Each section was broken into manageable steps. A progress bar or similar indicator showed the user‚Äôs current step within the overall SAQ. ‚ÄúSave‚Äù controls were conveniently located at the bottom, allowing users to save progress at any time.
  • Option 2 was designed so the user was taken directly into SAQ 1 upon entry. Progress through the three sections was visually tracked at the top of the interface. Each section included a concise description and helpful tips. Questions were displayed in a two-column layout on desktop for efficient completion. Given the complexity and potential for misinterpretation of some questions, helpful tooltips were provided beneath each one.

    Final outcome

    I delivered a redesigned first section of the SAQ, informed by user testing, workshops, and research. The new layout improved task clarity and engagement ‚Äî laying a foundation for scaling improvements across the full questionnaire. Getting the first section done was the hard part, but once that was in place and data was flowing, we could easily build out the other sections using the new components and feed everything back into the backend to generate insights.
