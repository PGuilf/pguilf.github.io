Redefining The Economist subscription journey

Jump to final outcome Close

I joined The Economist as part of a new dedicated future customer experience (FCX) team. The Economist identified many of their customers found the online experience disjointed and frustrating, leading many to unhappy churn.¬†

The goal was to implement a seamless, cohesive and streamlined end-to-end experience. An integral part of this, in line with the businesses objectives was to dramatically improve subscription sales. A key part of FCX was improving the subscription journey from selecting an offer to registering and checking out.

The Team

I was the principal product designer working on FCX alongside a dedicated PM, tech lead, scrum master and a business analyst. I worked with a large team of developers across two platforms; Salesforce and Limio which together created FCX.


Discovery: defining the work

I ran hour-long interviews with 16 users to explore the existing subscription journey. Insights were grouped into key stages: offer page, registration/checkout, and confirmation. I then affinity mapped common themes in Airtable and ran a workshop with stakeholders to prioritise solutions. Also using UserZoom, I then ran unmoderated tests with The Economist‚Äôs current and target audience to validate or challenge ideas. Playback videos, timings, and heatmaps helped us assess interaction quality and user behaviour. I was also fortunate to be able to use dovetail to look at The Economist extensive research repository.

Product ideas

From research, we were able to build out key personas and some high-level user journeys for existing, lapsed, and prospective subscribers. I then started to map out user flows of the current journeys and consider any potential pain points. Alongside this, I started to sketch ideas to explore options before moving into low-fidelity wireframes.

Design iteration and validation

I progressed into mid-fidelity designs and prototyped new journeys in Figma, refining with the development team. I ran another research round with current and lapsed subscribers, alongside rapid unmoderated usability testing in UserZoom. This helped validate decisions with key stakeholders. From there, I moved into and high-fidelity designs, applying the design system and its principles correctly.

Subscription offer pages

On the offer page I wanted a combined approach showing digital and print + digital together for a clearer comparison and individual pages for subscription types. Currently the most popular subscription was the print+digital quartley with 35.8% share of subscriptions followed by the digital Quartley with 35.2%. The key push from the business was to increase Annual subscriptions and digital only subscriptions.

From here we then put together prototypes. We presented these journeys and prototypes to the developers to establish any limitations. Vital, as the end product would be built across two platforms and we‚Äôd need to ensure they interacted correctly. A key component to this was IA in terms of labelling and design heirachy, we refined and tweak this based on user testing.

Registration

Registration clarity was a key focus, informed by competitor analysis, research and close collaboration with internal stakeholders to minimize potential friction.

Checkout

We went through multiple versions, both low and high fidelity, mapping out key areas and analysing historic data with the aim of simplifying and reducing friction. Related sequential steps were grouped logically (e.g., Shipping > Payment > Review), and key details like shipping options and costs were displayed early to minimise user effort. A progress bar was added initially to help users track their position in the process.

Over six months, we ran scrappy user tests to gather quick feedback, iterating as we went. We different information approaches versions with step by step across multiple pages, but the one-page version ultimately tested best. This approach had challenges, particularly with error handling when sections weren‚Äôt completed. To address this, we implemented a staggered approach, disabling parts of the form as users moved through each step to ensure they completed necessary fields. We also leveraged information from subscriber renewals to reduce friction at key points.

Using GA, we tracked drop-off points and conversion rates, refining based on real-time data. Hotjar helped identify where users dropped off, focusing our efforts on key friction points. After A/B testing, we achieved a 0.5% increase in conversion rates and a 6% reduction in drop-off time at checkout.

Confirmation

User testing had flagged a lack of clear feedback for the user after their purchase is complete. Details of their order being unclear was also a pain point. A requirement from the business was to create an MVP onboarding experience. Design wise I looked at the information architecture of the table and using typography weight / colours to give prominence to the vital information.

Onboarding

We then explored our post-onboarding experience, focusing on how to guide users into our platform and encourage engagement with features like newsletters, podcasts, and apps. At the time, we drew inspiration from streaming services like Disney+ and Netflix, mapping out user journeys after registration. And language schools like Babell and editorial like New York Times games.

We expected this approach to resonate with users, but testing showed otherwise. Often, users wanted to read a specific article or view a particular piece of content, and any additional friction wasn‚Äôt tested. While we considered an A/B test, the development effort was too high. Instead, we introduced a preferences section within the account area, providing a more seamless way for users to tailor their experience. We found strong engagement with this approach, and it was well received in user interviews.

My Account

I kicked off discovery for the account area, which had previously been limited to updating contact details. I reviewed flows across the platform to identify where functionality was causing friction and making it difficult for users to self-serve, leading to a high volume of support calls. I then centralised key functionality into the account area, focusing on subscription renewals (active and lapsed), contact details, newsletter preferences, FAQs, and customer support. This increased traffic to the account area, reduced friction across the site, particularly in onboarding, which had tested poorly during ideation and lowered the number of calls to customer support.

Refinements

Prior to a big promotional push I looked at how we could improve the offer page. I wanted to improve decision making time and increase Annual digital subscriptions. To achieve this I used typography with less weights / sizes and more space. This created a new hierarchy which put more emphasis on the pricing over the term. We also move Annual above Quarterly so this was pre-selected.
Results: Decision making time improved from an average of 03.23 to 02.58 minutes. Moving Annual above Quarterly didn't indicate to increase user selecting annual subscriptions. Further A/B testing was needed to properly test this hypothesis.
